The Modern Sculptors: Jonathan Anderson and Dario Vitale

Publicado em: Mar 30th 2026

While Homer sculpted heroes with words, Anderson and Vitale sculpt the human silhouette with fabric. One brings creative chaos (Anderson), the other brings classical order (Vitale). It’s a modern-day version of Apollo and Dionysus on the runway.

Anderson is a surrealist—truly the Kershaw of fashion—making the impossible seem possible. He understands fashion in a way that feels almost spiritual, but most importantly, he understands a vital truth: fashion is political.

In his debut, Anderson reimagined societal gender roles, proving he isn’t afraid of questioning the political status quo; he dares us to do the same. At Loewe, he often uses surrealistic elements to challenge our sense of reality. For Anderson, fashion is about two things: politics and fantasy. It is fascinating to see how he navigates both themes in his collections.

In June 2014, for his Loewe debut, Anderson dared to innovate. He shattered the idea that leather is inherently "heavy," exploring handcrafted work in a way that was both recreational and imaginative. Over the years, Anderson grew bolder, and that is where the true politics began. He brought humor to his creations, forcing us to question what is "beautiful" and what is "odd," poking at gender norms while embracing vibrant colors and extraordinary volumes. He slowly carved out the surrealist territory we know today.

Dario Vitale has a different approach. He is like Buehler’s fastballs: precise and elegant. When he debuted, Vitale shocked the world not just with the pieces presented, but with the intentional lack of "product."

Vitale, much like Anderson, made a statement: he wants to prove that Versace is more than just a brand trying to sell a product; he wants to prove that Versace IS art. In my opinion, he succeeded. Under his touch, projects were driven solely by artistic intent, proving that cultural relevance is essential to justify ambitious brand initiatives. In Vitale’s Versace lies the spirit of an indescribable humanity.

For Dario, this approach boils down to the marks of a life lived to its fullest—which is one of the greatest political statements one can make. Vitale also speaks of "maximalism," "mythology," and most importantly, sex—and the intense emotional experiences it evokes. As he told Vogue Brasil, he sees sex as a central factor in fashion’s ability to promote self-mastery. And as Sigmund Freud famously put it: everything is sexual.
